# Aligning Individuals with Environmental Health

This is the overarching problem we need to solve before we can truly combat climate change. We can try to limit pollution, ecosystem degradation, sprawl, bad agricultural practices, etc. through command and control policies, but if there is financial incentive to do such things, people will figure a way around such laws. People might outright break the law, move to a place where laws are more lax, lobby politicians, among many other options.

The only way we can move towards a future of natural abundance is to take advantage of market forces and ensure people are financially incentivized to take care of our planet. However, it's one thing to make such a statement and quite another to get the idea working in the real world.

Let me preface the rest of this conversation with the acknowledgement that this is a difficult problem to implement for several reasons:

1. Ideological thinking can inhibit rational thought for people in both pro-market and pro-environment camps. As humans, we are inherently biased, but we can overcome this by learning from people from all walks of life and different geographical origins.
2. The best path forward (in my opinion) is not to create sweeping changes and regulatory change, but rather through a few key changes that can act as the "lead domino". One example could be to introduce a global cap and trade system for CO<sub>2</sub> emissions for specific industries. However, many environmentalists (myself included) want to fix it all NOW. We are playing a long-term game that can't be won in a single fell swoop. 
3. Any approach MUST be a global approach. There have been several attempts for countries to unite against the looming climate catastrophes (such as the Paris Climate Accord), but there are several problems with these approaches. Chiefly, countries oftentimes have to make economic sacrifices to meet the agreement requirements. This leads to resistance and ultimately non-compliance.
4. The human-led destruction of the environment is happening on multiple fronts. Agriculture, urban/suburban development and sprawl, resource extraction (fossil fuels, logging, mining, etc.) and fishing are some of the key destructive human activities we can focus on. Because of this, only a multifaceted approach will successfully heal our environment and put us on a trajectory towards global biological health and flourishing.
5. Ecosystems are infinitely complicated and hard to measure. We are learning more every day about the complex interactions between different organisms in nature and how such interactions benefit an ecosystem as a whole. For this reason, accurately quantifying overall ecosystem health is a step that must be overcome. While boiling the _true_ health of an ecosystem to a single metric (or set of metrics) is unlikely in the short- to medium-term, it is possible to come up with good proxies based on our current understanding.

The purpose of this site and associated posts is to explore how to overcome these challenges so humans do, in fact, start trending in the direction of technological AND natural abundance.
